2. Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT)

The KMT explains the behaviour of gases using these ideas:

  • Gases are made of tiny particles in constant, random motion.
  • Particles are very far apart compared to their size.
  • They collide with each other and with the container walls — these collisions are elastic (no energy is lost).
  • Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles.
  • Absolute zero (0 K) = the temperature at which all particle motion stops.
Pressure (KMT view): when gas particles collide with the walls of the container, they push them. The total push per unit area is the pressure.
More collisions or stronger collisions → more pressure.

3. Pressure

Pressure is the force applied per unit of area.

P = F / S
  • F = force (N, newtons)
  • S = surface area (m²)
  • P = pressure (Pa, pascals = N/m²)
The same force over a smaller area produces greater pressure. That's why a sharp needle pierces easily but a finger does not.

Atmospheric pressure

The atmosphere is a thick layer of air around Earth. Its weight produces pressure on everything on the surface.

  • Standard atmospheric pressure at sea level = 1 atm = 101 325 Pa ≈ 760 mmHg ≈ 1013 mbar.
  • Pressure decreases with altitude (less air above us).

5. Boyle–Mariotte Law (P–V)

Describes the relationship between pressure and volume when temperature is constant.

If T is constant, P and V are inversely proportional.
When you compress a gas (V decreases), pressure increases. And vice versa.
P · V = constant   →   P₁ · V₁ = P₂ · V₂

6. Charles's Law (V–T)

Describes the relationship between volume and temperature when pressure is constant.

If P is constant, V and T are directly proportional.
When you heat a gas, it expands. When you cool it, it contracts.
V / T = constant   →   V₁ / T₁ = V₂ / T₂
⚠ Temperature must be in kelvin (K)!

7. Gay-Lussac's Law (P–T)

Describes the relationship between pressure and temperature when volume is constant.

If V is constant, P and T are directly proportional.
Heating a sealed container increases pressure; cooling decreases pressure.
P / T = constant   →   P₁ / T₁ = P₂ / T₂
⚠ Temperature must be in kelvin (K)!

8. Combined Gas Law

If all three variables (P, V, T) change at once, we combine the three previous laws:

(P₁ · V₁) / T₁ = (P₂ · V₂) / T₂

This is the most general formula and works for any gas as long as the amount of gas doesn't change.

If T is constant, T₁ = T₂ and the formula collapses to Boyle's law. If P is constant, you get Charles's law. If V is constant, you get Gay-Lussac's law.

9. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Boyle
A gas occupies 6 L at 2 atm. If the temperature does not change and we compress it to 1.5 L, what is the new pressure?
1) Identify law: T constant → Boyle.
2) Data: P₁ = 2 atm, V₁ = 6 L, V₂ = 1.5 L, P₂ = ?
3) P₁·V₁ = P₂·V₂ → 2 · 6 = P₂ · 1.5
4) P₂ = 12 / 1.5 = 8 atm
Example 2 — Charles
A balloon has 3 L of gas at 27 °C. What volume will it have at 127 °C, at the same pressure?
1) Identify law: P constant → Charles.
2) Convert: T₁ = 27 + 273 = 300 K, T₂ = 127 + 273 = 400 K.
3) V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ → 3/300 = V₂/400
4) V₂ = (3 · 400) / 300 = 4 L
Example 3 — Gay-Lussac (fire extinguisher)
A 3 L CO₂ cylinder withstands up to 15 atm. At 27 °C it is at 2 atm. What is the maximum temperature it can reach before bursting?
1) Identify law: V constant → Gay-Lussac.
2) Convert: T₁ = 27 + 273 = 300 K. P₁ = 2 atm, P₂ = 15 atm.
3) P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ → 2/300 = 15/T₂
4) T₂ = (15 · 300) / 2 = 2250 K → 2250 − 273 = 1977 °C
Example 4 — Unit conversion
Express 1140 mmHg in SI units (Pa).
1) Equivalence: 760 mmHg = 101 325 Pa.
2) 1140 mmHg · (101 325 Pa / 760 mmHg)
3) = 151 987.5 Pa ≈ 1.52 · 10⁵ Pa
