1. Chemical Reactions

A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances (reactants) are transformed into different substances (products).

REACTANTS → PRODUCTS
  • Atoms are not created or destroyed — they just rearrange.
  • Old bonds are broken; new bonds form.
  • The number and type of atoms must be the same on both sides.
Example — combustion of butane (camping gas):
2 C4H10 + 13 O2 → 8 CO2 + 10 H2O
Butane reacts with oxygen to form carbon dioxide and water.

2. Balancing Chemical Equations

To balance a reaction we add coefficients (numbers in front of the formulas) so that the number of atoms of each element is equal on both sides.

⚠ You can only change coefficients, never the subscripts inside the formulas. Changing the subscript changes the substance!

Step-by-step method

  • Count atoms of each element on both sides.
  • Balance metals first, then non-metals, then H, then O last.
  • If you need a fraction, multiply all coefficients to clear it.
  • Check the final count.

3. Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier)

In a chemical reaction, the total mass of the reactants equals the total mass of the products.

This is true because atoms are not destroyed — they only rearrange. The number of atoms of each element on the left equals the number on the right.

m (reactants) = m (products)

4. Proust's Law of Definite Proportions

When two or more elements combine to form a compound, they always do so in a fixed mass ratio.

For example, water is always 2 g of H : 16 g of O (ratio 1 : 8). It doesn't matter where the water comes from — rain, sea, tap, or a chemical reaction — the proportion is always the same.

This is why the same equation always gives the same mass ratios between reactants and products — and that's what allows us to do stoichiometry calculations.

5. Molar Mass

The molar mass (M) of a substance is the mass (in grams) of one mole of that substance. It is obtained by adding the atomic masses of all the atoms in its formula.

M(compound) = Σ (atomic mass × number of atoms)

Examples

  • H₂O: 2·1 + 16 = 18 g/mol
  • CO₂: 12 + 2·16 = 44 g/mol
  • O₂: 2·16 = 32 g/mol
  • C₄H₁₀ (butane): 4·12 + 10·1 = 58 g/mol
  • MgO: 24.3 + 16 = 40.3 g/mol
  • NH₃: 14 + 3·1 = 17 g/mol

6. Stoichiometric Mass Calculations

Knowing the balanced equation and the molar masses, we can calculate the mass of any reactant or product if we know one of them.

The 3 steps

  1. Write and balance the chemical equation.
  2. Calculate molar masses and write the stoichiometry in grams.
  3. Use a conversion factor (proportion / rule of three) to find the unknown.

7. Worked Examples

Example 1 — Magnesium burning
Magnesium reacts with oxygen to form magnesium oxide. Calculate: (a) the mass of oxygen needed to react with 8 g of Mg; (b) the mass of MgO produced.
1) Equation: 2 Mg + O₂ → 2 MgO.
2) Molar masses: M(Mg)=24.3, M(O₂)=32, M(MgO)=40.3.
3) Stoichiometry in grams: 2·24.3 = 48.6 g Mg + 32 g O₂ → 2·40.3 = 80.6 g MgO.
4) (a) 8 g Mg · (32 g O₂ / 48.6 g Mg) = 5.3 g O₂.
5) (b) 8 g Mg · (80.6 g MgO / 48.6 g Mg) = 13.3 g MgO.
Check (mass conservation): 8 + 5.3 = 13.3 ✅
Example 2 — Ammonia decomposition
Ammonia decomposes: NH₃ → N₂ + H₂. Calculate how many grams of ammonia decompose to produce 7 g of N₂, and the mass of hydrogen produced.
1) Balanced: 2 NH₃ → N₂ + 3 H₂.
2) Molar masses: M(NH₃)=17, M(N₂)=28, M(H₂)=2.
3) Stoichiometry in grams: 2·17 = 34 g NH₃ → 28 g N₂ + 3·2 = 6 g H₂.
4) NH₃ needed: 7 g N₂ · (34 g NH₃ / 28 g N₂) = 8.5 g NH₃.
5) H₂ produced: 7 g N₂ · (6 g H₂ / 28 g N₂) = 1.5 g H₂.
Check: 8.5 = 7 + 1.5 ✅
Example 3 — Butane combustion
How many grams of CO₂ are produced when 29 g of butane burn completely?
1) 2 C₄H₁₀ + 13 O₂ → 8 CO₂ + 10 H₂O.
2) M(C₄H₁₀)=58, M(CO₂)=44.
3) Stoichiometry: 2·58 = 116 g butane → 8·44 = 352 g CO₂.
4) 29 g · (352 / 116) = 88 g CO₂.
